Career Accomplishments

The last 15 years of my career were spent at just two companies, showcasing my loyalty to employers. I believe that by staying with one employer, I can continuously improve my knowledge of the business, not just the technology, and deliver an ever-increasing amount of value to the business.

When I joined athenahealth, our data center network was what I’d call technically elegant, but dangerously fragile. It followed what we jokingly referred to as the “upside-down tree” architecture — all devices joined into a single virtual chassis using proprietary features of our incumbent vendor’s equipment. It made day-to-day tasks easier, sure. But when one device glitched, the whole network could (and did) go down. We were one failure away from complete data center outages.At the same time, the company was growing. Fast. A wave of new customers had just signed on, and the infrastructure needed to keep up. The compute capacity demand was exploding — but expanding under the existing network design would be both risky and expensive. Our vendor was in the “premium pricing” category, and the cost of expansion was four times what we could afford.That’s when I took ownership — not just of the architecture, but of the outcome.I spent several months deeply researching modern network architectures, interviewing vendors, conducting proof-of-concepts, and challenging assumptions. I eventually led the re-architecture of the production data center network, inspired by a next-gen design pioneered by Facebook. The new design enabled virtually endless horizontal scalabilityand dramatically increased throughput — all using off-the-shelf hardware instead of high-margin proprietary systems.Of course, it wasn’t a clean arc. There were moments of uncertainty, changes in direction, tough vendor conversations, and long nights wondering if we had made the right call. At times, I questioned my sanity for staking a billion-dollar SaaS platform on a bold new architecture. I’m grateful every day to my then-manager — who gave me the freedom to lead, the mentorship to improve, and the encouragement to keep going when things felt overwhelming.But this wasn’t just a solo effort. As the Director of Infrastructure, I had the privilege of leading a team of incredible engineers who made this happen:

They built the deployment schedule and orchestrated installs across multiple data centers

They created automation solutions to streamline repetitive tasks and eliminate human error

They provided business and after-hours support, keeping the transition invisible to our users

They rapidly learned new vendor technologies, often without formal training

And they earned some well-deserved bragging rights when they presented their work at our internal developer conference

We didn’t just build a better network. We created a moment of shared pride, ownership, and technical excellence.The results were clear:

40% increase in backbone throughput

Dramatically lower cost per port — at a quarter of the original vendor’s quote

Improved observability, failover, and resilience

And most importantly: we were ready before the customer growth wave hit

The architecture we put in place served the company reliably for five years — well beyond initial expectations. Eventually, the market caught up, and vendor pricing leveled out. But by then, we had already saved millions, built confidence in our engineering leadership, and proven that infrastructure — when led with clarity and courage — can be a strategic weapon, not just a utility.This project wasn't just about ports and protocols. It was about earning trust, aligning deeply with the business, and leading a team through change, uncertainty, and breakthrough.It’s still one of the things I’m most proud of.

At athenahealth, as the network grew rapidly in size, in large part thanks to the earlier-mentioned architecture changes, the need for a central management solution became painfully evident. After all, Athenahealth was still used to managing the whole data center from a single CLI, even if that meant the whole data center was managed as one virtual device. At the time, this single management point was something out of a dream for the vast majority of computer networks.  The norm was what was known as "box-by-box" management. As a result of the spine-and-leaf architecture, Athenahealth now had 100x more boxes to manage. Box-by-box wasn't going to cut it anymore. To combat this problem, I introduced central network configuration control into the new architecture. This was not based on making all devices appear as one virtual device seen in the previous architecture. Instead, after several proof-of-concept projects, I selected Puppet as the configuration management and automation solution. Puppet was based on an agent running on every piece of equipment and locally enforcing the desired configuration state of that hardware. Furthermore, server admins have already been using Puppet to manage their immense fleet of servers, so selecting Puppet also presented an opportunity to unify data center configuration management under one automation framework. Unfortunately, the unification part never happened, as it proved too complex to realize the benefits. Introducing Puppet into the network configuration management reduced human errors and enabled engineers to acquire new and valuable automation skills. It also standardized configurations, uncovered mismanaged "pockets" on the network, and eased audit and compliance. Together with my team of network engineers, we were able to allow the executive leadership to feel confident about the quality of the production network, ease compliance with annual audits, which are required for an organization of this profile, and get some bragging rights around being at the forefront of technological progress. I even guest-starred on a couple of episodes of Packet Pushers - a popular network engineering podcast.

Just as I joined athenahealth, they were figuring out how to save money on the "last mile". The "last mile" is a telecommunication term describing the final stretch of a network circuit enabling the delivery of connectivity to a site. Normally, the long-haul portion is provided directly by the vendor contracted, and the local access is subcontracted to the local "monopoly." The result of this is the last mile cost, in many instances, is over 50% of the total cost of the whole service. When I joined the company, I took the problem at hand, the handful of good ideas already on the table, and spearheaded the deployment of a fiber-optic network connecting the main campus, local Internet Exchange, and production data center. This included learning new technologies like DWDM and VxLAN, which was in its infancy at the time, evaluating vendors capable of providing it most stably, and designing, building, and operationalizing the solution to create significant cost savings in providing Internet connectivity to the data center and campus. The cost savings were so dramatic that the project broke even 18 months later. As far as I know, the solution is still in use today and is one of the systems requiring the fewest changes and experiencing the fewest total outages.

Shortly after I joined Sovereign Bank - an east coast thrift bank, it was acquired by Santander Bank - a global juggernaut headquartered in Madrid, Spain, and by many accounts, the most valuable banking brand in the world. The need for operational transformation in all areas of IT was palpable. In particular, their branch network was built based on old VPN architecture, dedicated and expensive private lines, aging hardware, and required box-by-box management. Moreover, any outage required "boots on the ground," which usually resulted in the branch closure for the day, possibly even two - not a good look for a global bank. Along with the support of my manager and mentor, I have designed and built a new branch network based on dynamic VPN running over the public broadband Internet rather than dedicated and costly private circuits. I added cellular connectivity capabilities, which enabled the branch to stay open even when the primary connectivity failed. I also simplified the deployment procedure so that an untrained branch staff member could assist in troubleshooting and service recovery efforts, thus eliminating the need to dispatch a qualified technician in most cases.

Most recently, at athenahealth I was tasked with delivering production-ready Kubernetes infrastructure on top of AWS Outpost platform. The existing leader was transitioning to a different role, and I was asked to step in and deliver the solution. Delivering this was business-critical as a major transformation effort depended on this delivery's success, so failure was not an option. In just a short 3 months with a team of 4 engineers spread between two continents, we have defined the project success criteria, built a plan of action, and, using agile practices, delivered a production-ready solution in time for the next phase of the global business transformation initiative. While the technology portion was not very challenging, the team management presented a slew of difficulties. There were personality clashes within the team, power struggles, fights for the spotlight, and timezone challenges. Given the short timeline and all the internal team dynamics I inherited, we could still cut through all that and execute. I believe this was possible due to my trusting in the expertise of the engineers and concentrating solely on conflict resolution within the team.

There were many other accomplishments, the details of which are fading from memory at this point, but if there is one theme throughout all of them, it's optimization. I love driving standards, compliance, and efficiency.

What's your story?

I grew up in motion. My father was in the military, and we moved often — across cities, climates, and contradictions. Like him, I kept drifting south. Eventually, we settled in Krasnodar, in southern Russia, where I lived from age 13 to 20. It wasn’t orderly — quite the opposite. It was a place full of noise, contradictions, and informal systems. But maybe that’s exactly why I became so drawn to clarity. I wanted things to make sense — and that became the lens I carried forward. In 2001, I first visited the United States — and I was instantly captivated. It felt like the future. Two years later, I returned with $200 in my pocket and a fierce desire to build a life. I took a job waiting tables at a local Mexican restaurant, often walking home with just $25 in tips. But I kept showing up. I kept learning. After moving to the U.S. full time, the pattern of movement continued — from Wisconsin to Boston to the Bay Area. Always south. Always toward warmth. My real start in technology came at a data center in Madison, Wisconsin, called 5NinesData. I was hired to do everything: Linux, Windows, networking, radios, power, cabling. That place became my alma mater. It didn’t just teach me tech — it taught me systems, infrastructure, and how to bring order to complexity. I’ll always be grateful to Todd Streicher, who took a chance on me. I later took a role at Epic Systems — a major healthcare software company — and not long after, made a move to Boston that opened the next chapter. After a few stepping-stone roles, I was hired at Sovereign Bank by Brad Rightmyer — a career-shaping enabler. Brad gave me a shot. At the bank, I helped design and support global infrastructure. I lived through the acquisition by Santander, learned how tech, politics, and culture intertwine, and made my first international business trips. When Brad left, Julio Romero became my manager — and eventually, my friend. Julio and I had similar roots — both systems thinkers, both from networking. He once told me, “You don’t have to participate in every single conversation.” That advice shaped me. I stopped trying to speak first, and started learning how to listen longer. That shift would define how I lead. After four years at Santander, something shifted. I couldn’t quite name it — but the culture felt different. Around that time, I was headhunted by athenahealth. The title was better, the salary stronger, and the work looked complex in all the right ways. I don’t remember who technically hired me — maybe Andy St. John, maybe Brian Lubelczyk — but both were instrumental, and I remain grateful to them for it. At athenahealth, I spent almost a decade shaping infrastructure from the inside out. I modernized networks, enabled hybrid cloud, drove cost reduction, implemented automation, improved compliance, and coached engineers through transitions. I dabbled in people management, led architecture forums, gave internal talks, lived through multiple layoffs — and eventually got caught in one. But the work mattered, and I know I made it better than I found it. Then life moved again. After eight years in the Bay Area, I relocated to Orange County, California, where I now live with my 16-month-old son. This is the farthest south I’ve ever lived. It’s also the place I dreamed of as a teenager — the palm trees, the coastline, the quiet promise of California. Back then, I’d only seen it in movies. Now, I walk those streets with my son. Life has a way of folding like that. Today, I work with technical leaders and teams as a strategic advisor, clarity coach, and infrastructure architect. I help them think clearly, scale wisely, and make decisions with confidence. I bring systems thinking, deep presence, and quiet care into complex environments — because I believe that every problem has a calm, workable solution. You just have to be patient enough to find it. What I value most now is being thoughtful and helpful. That’s the through-line of it all. And if someone looks back years from now and says, “He helped me think more clearly — helped me get where I didn’t think I could go,” I’ll know I spent my time well.

Empathy. It took me a long time to progress from "How dare you think or do any other way than mine" to "I have no earthly idea what your life is like, and I trust you are doing your very best."
