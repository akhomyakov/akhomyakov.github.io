016: At athenahealth, as the network grew rapidly in size, in large part thanks to the earlier-mentioned architecture changes, the need for a central management solution became painfully evident. After all, Athenahealth was still used to managing the whole data center from a single CLI, even if that meant the whole data center was managed as one virtual device. At the time, this single management point was something out of a dream for the vast majority of computer networks.  The norm was what was known as "box-by-box" management. As a result of the spine-and-leaf architecture, Athenahealth now had 100x more boxes to manage. Box-by-box wasn't going to cut it anymore. To combat this problem, I introduced central network configuration control into the new architecture. This was not based on making all devices appear as one virtual device seen in the previous architecture. Instead, after several proof-of-concept projects, I selected Puppet as the configuration management and automation solution. Puppet was based on an agent running on every piece of equipment and locally enforcing the desired configuration state of that hardware. Furthermore, server admins have already been using Puppet to manage their immense fleet of servers, so selecting Puppet also presented an opportunity to unify data center configuration management under one automation framework. Unfortunately, the unification part never happened, as it proved too complex to realize the benefits. Introducing Puppet into the network configuration management reduced human errors and enabled engineers to acquire new and valuable automation skills. It also standardized configurations, uncovered mismanaged "pockets" on the network, and eased audit and compliance. Together with my team of network engineers, we were able to allow the executive leadership to feel confident about the quality of the production network, ease compliance with annual audits, which are required for an organization of this profile, and get some bragging rights around being at the forefront of technological progress. I even guest-starred on a couple of episodes of Packet Pushers - a popular network engineering podcast.

017: Just as I joined athenahealth, they were figuring out how to save money on the "last mile". The "last mile" is a telecommunication term describing the final stretch of a network circuit enabling the delivery of connectivity to a site. Normally, the long-haul portion is provided directly by the vendor contracted, and the local access is subcontracted to the local "monopoly." The result of this is the last mile cost, in many instances, is over 50% of the total cost of the whole service. When I joined the company, I took the problem at hand, the handful of good ideas already on the table, and spearheaded the deployment of a fiber-optic network connecting the main campus, local Internet Exchange, and production data center. This included learning new technologies like DWDM and VxLAN, which was in its infancy at the time, evaluating vendors capable of providing it most stably, and designing, building, and operationalizing the solution to create significant cost savings in providing Internet connectivity to the data center and campus. The cost savings were so dramatic that the project broke even 18 months later. As far as I know, the solution is still in use today and is one of the systems requiring the fewest changes and experiencing the fewest total outages.
